# Voice

## Core principle
Write like a senior consultant talking to another senior person across
a table. Not like a marketing site. Not like a LinkedIn influencer.

## Sentence rules

1. Short sentences. Fragments OK. Long sentences only when the
   thought genuinely needs them.

2. No "X doesn't just Y, it Z" patterns. Or any em-dash structure
   that creates artificial drama. Examples to ban:
   - "A strong SAP team doesn't just set up a system, it makes sure it works."
   - "We don't just deliver projects, we deliver outcomes."
   - "It's not just consulting, it's partnership."

3. No "transformation journey" language. No "unlock", "leverage",
   "synergy", "best-in-class", "world-class", "game-changer",
   "revolutionize", "empower", "seamless", "robust", "cutting-edge".

4. Drop connective words that make sentences sound formal.
   "Furthermore", "additionally", "moreover", "in conclusion",
   "it is worth noting that". Just say the next sentence.

5. First person. Always. "I led", "I worked with", "I delivered".
   Not "we", "the team", "Quantinoid". Personal brand site.
   Exception: testimonials and external quotes.

6. Concrete over abstract. Names, numbers, places, dates, modules.
   Don't say "a major airline", say "Etihad". Don't say "a complex
   migration", say "ECC to S/4HANA across 25 entities".

7. Sentence-case headings. Not Title Case. Not ALL CAPS.
   "How I work", not "How I Work" and not "HOW I WORK".

## Words to favour
- "I", "you" (when addressing the reader)
- "Programme", "engagement", "delivery" (over "project" when scope is large)
- "Mid-flight", "post-go-live", "blueprint" (industry-specific clarity)
- "Finance close", "day rate", "scope creep" (CEO vocabulary)
- "Brownfield", "greenfield", "selective" (technical clarity)

## Words to avoid
- "Solutions" as a noun (use "tools", "products", "approaches")
- "Stakeholders" (use specific roles: CIO, CFO, sponsor)
- "Innovative", "innovative AI", "innovation" (empty)
- "End-to-end" (overused, often inaccurate)
- "Holistic" (corporate bingo)
- "Ecosystem" (unless literally accurate)
- "Strategic" as filler
- "AI-powered", "AI-driven" (everyone says this)

## Patterns I like

Stating a problem then resolving it.
> Most ERP programmes fail. Yours doesn't have to.

Two short sentences with a pivot.
> Direct involvement. No junior team learning on your budget.

Numbers without buildup.
> 25 entities. One ERP. $60M saved.

Conditional with consequence.
> If your SI is writing your business case, you've already lost.

## Patterns I don't like

Buildup with payoff that overpromises.
> "After 25 years in this industry, what I've come to realise is..."

Faux-humble brag.
> "I'm proud to say that..."

Three-item lists with parallel structure that sound generated.
> "We bring clarity, capability, and confidence."

## Headings I'd write
- "Most ERP programmes fail. Yours doesn't have to."
- "How I work."
- "What I believe."
- "Programmes I've led."
- "Other things I do."

## Headings I wouldn't write
- "Driving Digital Transformation Through Strategic Innovation"
- "Why Choose Noel?"
- "Our Comprehensive Suite of Solutions"
- "Empowering Your Business Journey"

## Length guide
- Hero subhead: 25-40 words.
- Section intro: 20-50 words.
- Card body: 15-30 words per bullet.
- Case study summary: 50-80 words.
- About / bio: 100-200 words on homepage. Full version on /about.

## Formatting
- Avoid heavy bolding. One or two phrases per page max.
- Bullets only when items are genuinely parallel. Otherwise prose.
- Avoid emoji on the public site. They're fine in Claude Code chat.
- Avoid Oxford commas if possible (consistency matters more than rule).

## Anti-AI-tells (for blog and long-form content)

Things that flag content as AI-generated to readers and to Google's
helpful content classifiers. Avoid these patterns.

### Banned openers
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In an era of digital transformation..."
- "As businesses increasingly..."
- "In the rapidly evolving landscape of..."
- "It's no secret that..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Picture this:"

### Banned transitions and connectives
- "Furthermore"
- "Moreover"
- "Additionally"
- "It's important to note that"
- "It's worth mentioning"
- "That being said"
- "With that in mind"
- "In conclusion"

### Banned closers
- "In summary, [restating everything just said]"
- "By following these steps, you can..."
- "Hopefully this guide helped you..."
- "Remember that the key to success is..."

### Structural tells
- Every section header followed by a 3-item bullet list with parallel
  grammar.
- Every paragraph the same length (3-4 sentences).
- Section titles like "What is X?", "Why X matters", "How to do X",
  "Benefits of X", "Conclusion". This pattern is a giveaway when used
  unbroken.
- Equal weighting of all subtopics. Real writers spend 5x as long on
  the bit they actually care about.

### Lexical tells
- "Delve" — banned. Hard banned. The word that broke AI detection in 2024.
- "Tapestry", "landscape", "realm", "sphere", "arena" used metaphorically.
- "Robust", "seamless", "innovative", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class".
- "It's crucial to understand"
- "Plays a pivotal role"
- "A testament to"
- Frequent use of "leverage" as a verb.

### Things that signal real human experience (do these)
- Specific dates: "In Q3 2023..."
- Specific cities/sites: "...at the Abu Dhabi office..."
- Named systems with versions: "S/4HANA 1909", "ECC 6.0 EHP 7"
- Named modules: "FI/CO", "MM/SD", "PP-PI"
- A surprised moment: "I assumed X. It turned out to be Y."
- An admission: "I got this wrong on the Etihad project."
- An aside: "(this is the part nobody tells you in vendor decks)"
- A judgment: "I'd do this differently next time."
- Sensory or physical detail: "The whiteboard from that workshop is
  still in the war room."
