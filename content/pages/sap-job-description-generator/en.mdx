## Job Description Techniques

The SAP Job Description Generator incorporates proven techniques from my 20+ years of [SAP implementation](https://noeldcosta.com/sap-implementation-vs-rollout-differences-challenges-best-practices/) experience. These methods help create descriptions that attract qualified candidates while filtering out those who lack essential skills:

### 1\. Precise technical requirements

Specifies exact module experience rather than general SAP knowledge. Instead of "SAP experience," use ["SAP MM configuration experience with Purchase Order processing](https://noeldcosta.com/sap-sd-sales-and-distribution/) and Inventory Management.

### 2\. Quantifiable experience

Includes measurable requirements that candidates can objectively assess. For example, "Minimum of 2 full lifecycle [SAP S/4HANA implementations"](https://noeldcosta.com/best-sap-implementation-strategies-to-avoid-costly-mistakes/) rather than "extensive implementation experience.

### 3\. Implementation phase clarity

[Defines which project](https://noeldcosta.com/sap-project-scope-template-management-and-control/) phases candidates should have experience with. This distinguishes between those who have only worked in support roles versus full implementation experience.

### 4\. Functional/technical balance

Clearly separates functional and technical requirements, especially important for hybrid roles like ABAP developers with functional knowledge.

### 5\. Behavioral indicators

Incorporates specific scenarios candidates should be able to handle based on actual project challenges rather than generic skills.

The effectiveness of these techniques has been proven across multiple [SAP](https://noeldcosta.com/project-planning-and-control-get-sap-projects-back-on-track/) implementations where the right talent made the critical difference between project success and failure.

## Key Metrics for Evaluating Job Decription Effectiveness

I have spent over 20 years watching companies struggle with [SAP hiring](https://noeldcosta.com/sap-implementation-vs-rollout-differences-challenges-best-practices/). The job descriptions they create either set them up for success or create problems that plague their implementations for years. It is about making the right calls at the very beginning of the recruitment process.

Here is the statistic you might have heard—43% of [SAP implementation](https://noeldcosta.com/sap-implementation-vs-rollout-differences-challenges-best-practices/) delays are directly tied to staffing the wrong team members because of poor job requirements. When I help clients evaluate their job description effectiveness, I focus on these key metrics:

-   **Application quality ratio:** Measure how many applications actually match your core requirements. If you are getting hundreds of applications but only 5% qualify, your job description is failing.
-   **Time-to-hire timeline:** Track how long it takes to fill positions. Good descriptions cut hiring time by weeks because they attract the right candidates faster.
-   **Technical assessment success:** Look at how many candidates who apply can actually pass your technical evaluations. This directly reflects how well your job description communicated the real requirements.
-   **Early performance indicators:** Monitor how quickly new hires become productive. The right job description sets proper expectations that translate to faster onboarding.
-   **Retention metrics:** Watch how many new hires leave within the first year. This often reveals mismatches between what the job description promised and the actual role.

If you look at [SAP implementations](https://noeldcosta.com/best-sap-implementation-strategies-to-avoid-costly-mistakes/) in different industries, the metrics you should focus on will vary. Financial sector [implementations need to emphasize compliance experience in ways](https://noeldcosta.com/erp-implementation-kpis-metrics/) manufacturing does not.

My bottom line after all these years: What seems like unnecessary detail in job descriptions saves you headaches, time, and money later on. That is not consultant-speak—that is hard-learned reality from someone who has seen it before and wants to help.
