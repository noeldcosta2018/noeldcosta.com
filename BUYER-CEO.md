# Buyer profile — Enterprise CEO / CIO / CFO

This is the primary buyer. Every word on the homepage gets written for them.

## Who they are

- Title: CIO, CFO, Chief Digital Officer, Programme Director,
  Group Head of Transformation
- Age: 45-58
- Company size: $100M-$5B revenue. Multi-entity. Often multi-country.
- Industry: Manufacturing, aviation, defence, energy, financial services,
  public sector. Anywhere with complex operations and regulatory weight.
- Geography: GCC, UK, Europe, occasional South Asia. Anglophone or
  fluent English in business setting.
- Background: 20+ years in enterprise tech, finance, or operations.
  Often grew up in Big 4, big SI, or in-house at a Fortune 500.
- Comp: $300K-$1.2M total. Equity sometimes. Career risk over salary.

## What they're trying to do

- Run a major ERP programme without it ending their career.
- Get AI working on top of ERP, but not look like they're chasing hype.
- Reduce dependency on the SI partner who's now charging change-orders
  monthly.
- Show the board they have the right advisor in the room.
- Move from operating in spreadsheets to operating from a system.

## What keeps them up

- The programme has slipped twice. The board is asking why.
- The SI says "scope creep". The team says "they oversold."
  No one has the truth.
- Finance close is still 14 days. AI was supposed to fix this.
- A peer at another company just had their CIO fired after a failed
  S/4HANA cutover. They read about it on LinkedIn.
- They don't know what they don't know.

## What they value (in order)

1. Demonstrated track record at their scale.
   They want to see EDGE, Etihad, ADNOC, PIF — companies they recognise.
   Mid-market case studies don't help.

2. Finance and operations depth.
   Most consultants understand the system. Few understand the close
   cycle, working capital, dual-ledger reporting, regulatory requirements.
   This is the differentiator.

3. Senior involvement that lasts.
   They've been burned by "the senior partner shows up for the pitch
   and disappears." They want the named human, in the room, for the
   duration.

4. Independence from vendors and SIs.
   They want someone whose paycheck doesn't depend on Activate hours
   or BTP licence sales.

5. A clear engagement model.
   What does month one look like. What's the deliverable. What's the
   commercial. They're not buying time. They're buying outcomes.

## How they make decisions

- They scan the homepage for under 90 seconds on first visit.
- They look at logos, named clients, size of programmes.
- They read the founder's About section.
- They check LinkedIn.
- They watch one or two YouTube videos.
- Then they book a call, or they don't.

If logos and case study scale don't match their world, they bounce
in 20 seconds.

## What they're scanning for on the homepage

In priority order:

1. Logos of companies their size. (Trust bar.)
2. Named clients and named programmes. (Case studies.)
3. Numbers — money, scale, scope. (Stats per case study.)
4. Independence cue. (Not a Big 4. Not branded SI. Personal brand.)
5. Engagement model. (How I work section.)
6. Senior credibility. (CIMA, AICPA, Masters in Accounting.)
7. Opinions. (What I believe — moves them from "competent" to
   "the right person".)

What they don't care about on first visit:
- AI capability detail (until they trust)
- Tools and products (interesting later, not now)
- YouTube channel (consumed after they decide to engage)
- Career advice content (signals wrong audience to them)

## Language they use

- "Programme" not "project" (when scope is enterprise)
- "Cutover", "go-live", "hypercare"
- "SI", "system integrator", "Big 4"
- "Steering Committee", "SteerCo", "Programme Board"
- "Business case", "operating model"
- "Close cycle", "month-end", "consolidation"
- "Risk", "audit", "compliance"
- "Brownfield", "greenfield", "selective" (when discussing migration)

Language they don't use (avoid in copy directed at them):
- "Solutions" as a noun
- "Journey"
- "Empower"
- "Disruption"
- "Game-changer"
- "Synergies"

## What makes them book a 30-min call

- They saw a logo they recognised.
- The case study size matches their context.
- The founder is named, accessible, and senior.
- The engagement model isn't opaque.
- One opinion in "What I believe" matched something they've thought
  themselves but couldn't say out loud.

## What makes them bounce in 20 seconds

- Generic stock photos of people in suits.
- "Driving Digital Transformation Through Innovation" headline.
- Mid-market client logos when they're an enterprise.
- A pricing table. (They're not buying a SaaS subscription.)
- A career advice section above the fold.
- Anything that looks like a CV in disguise.

## How they'll engage

- 30-minute discovery call (Calendly).
- Followed by a paid scoping engagement (1-2 weeks, day rate or fixed).
- Followed by a longer engagement (3-12 months, fee structure varies
  by scope).

The homepage isn't trying to close a sale. It's trying to win the
30-minute call.
